yersinia — parameter & prior explainer

A parameter-by-parameter guide for fitting plague models with the yersinia package. For each parameter: biological meaning, units, what the prebuilt scenarios use, what prior to put on it when you fit it, my own judgement based on the historical / clinical literature, and whether it’s typically fixed, shared, or per-outbreak local.

All rates are per day. Carcass-based transmission formulation (Didelot et al. 2017). States: rats S, I, R, Q; humans S_h, I_h, R_h, D_h.

How to read each parameter card
Shared
Biology that doesn’t change between outbreaks. Single value across the cohort.
Local
Outbreak-specific. One value per outbreak in a hierarchical fit.
Fixed
Pinned at the scenario or metadata value; rarely fit.
Rarely fit
Identifiable in theory; mortality-only data won’t tell you. Leave fixed unless you have side information.

Disease transmission how plague moves between hosts

beta_rcarcass-to-rat transmission rateper day

Mass-action rate at which susceptible rats become infected from contact with infectious carcasses (Q). Combined with rho via lambda_r = beta_r · Q · (1 − exp(−rho · T_r / K_r)) / T_r. Drives R0 on the rat side: R0 ≈ beta_r · (1 − g_r) · (1 − exp(−rho)) / delta_R.

scenariodefaultsdidelothistoricalkeeling-gilliganmodern-estimates
value0.770.7710.08630.9
Default prior: Uniform(min = 0.1, max = 3)
My take

Plague R0 in the historical literature clusters in the 1.5–3 range (Eyam 1.6–2.2, Cairo 1801 ~2.85, Black-Death cities 2–3). The wider Uniform(0.1, 3.0) here lets the chain explore weak-transmission regimes that fail to take off (diagnostic of mis-specification) and high-transmission regimes for rare super-spreading scenarios. Biologically, anything above 1.5 implies very rapid rat-to-rat propagation; this is plausible in densely packed urban warehouse settings but not in scattered village rat populations. A more principled prior is to sample R0 ∼ Gamma(3, 0.6) and convert to beta_r via with_R0_to_beta_r(), which is what the Barcelona hierarchical vignette does.

Shared
beta_hcarcass-to-human transmission rateper day

Force-of-infection coefficient from carcasses (Q) to susceptible humans. lambda_h = beta_h · Q · exp(−rho · T_r / K_r) / K_r. The exp(−rho · T_r / K_r) term is the fraction of fleas that have given up looking for rats and start biting humans — strongest when rat populations crash mid-outbreak.

scenariodefaultsdidelothistoricalkeeling-gilliganmodern-estimates
value0.020.01450.022.74e-050.017
Default prior: Uniform(min = 0.001, max = 0.5)
My take

Almost certainly varies more than the rat-side biology does — it folds in housing density, sanitation, plague awareness, and human flea-bite tolerance. Didelot's posterior for Cairo 1801 is 0.0145 (CI [0.005, 0.046]); Black-Death estimates push higher (~0.1+) because medieval European cities were rat-dense and flea-exposed. The default Uniform(0.001, 0.5) matches the seasonal vignette and covers Eyam through Givry-style Black-Death outbreaks. If posterior pins against 0.5, that's evidence the bubonic-only pathway is insufficient and the outbreak needs pneumonic transmission (beta_I) in the fitted set. The lower bound near zero is non-negotiable: in modern well-ventilated settings, carcass-to-human transmission is essentially zero.

Local
beta_Ihuman-to-human transmission rateper day

Direct human-to-human transmission, dominant in pneumonic plague (respiratory droplet pathway). lambda_hh = beta_I · I_h / T_h. Set to zero in pure-bubonic scenarios.

scenariodefaultsdidelothistoricalkeeling-gilliganmodern-estimates
value00.02150.0300.01
Default prior: Uniform(min = 0, max = 0.1)
My take

Most well-recorded plague outbreaks were predominantly bubonic, with at most a small pneumonic minority. Didelot's posterior of 0.0215 contributes ~18% of Cairo 1801 cases — consistent with a small pneumonic component. The 1910–11 Manchuria epidemic was the canonical 100% pneumonic outbreak with beta_I > 1/day and case-fatality near 100%. For your average Bills-of-Mortality outbreak: leave fixed at 0 unless you have line-list evidence of household clusters or rapid secondary attacks within days of an index case.

Fixed
rhocarcass infectivity rangedimensionless

Controls how quickly fleas "give up" on a dead rat and look for a new host. (1 − exp(−rho · T_r / K_r)) is the fraction of fleas that have found a new rat host; the complement exp(−rho · T_r / K_r) is the fraction that bite humans. Larger rho ⇒ fleas redistribute fast, less time biting humans. Not a spatial "range" despite the name.

scenariodefaultsdidelothistoricalkeeling-gilliganmodern-estimates
value2.52.632103
Default prior: Uniform(min = 0.3, max = 6)
My take

Biologically informed by Xenopsylla cheopis behavior — the canonical plague flea. A starved flea can survive 1–6 weeks off-host (laboratory; Bacot-Martin curves), but in practice seeks a new host within days. Translating that to rho is fraught because rho also folds in the rat-density-dependent encounter rate. K&G's rho = 10 reflects their fine-spatial coarse-graining (small block = high rat density), not biology — don't transplant it to outbreak fits. For most population-level fits, 2–3 is the consensus; if forced to pin, 2.5 is the safe choice. The prior is wide because rho is weakly identified from death-count data alone — it couples with beta_r and K_r in complicated ways.

Carcass dynamics how long Q sticks around

delta_Rcarcass decay rateper day

Rate at which infectious rat carcasses become non-infectious (fleas die off, body decomposes). 1 / delta_R is the mean carcass infectious period. Temperature- and humidity-sensitive; the seasonal vector multiplies this rate to model wet/dry season effects.

scenariodefaultsdidelothistoricalkeeling-gilliganmodern-estimates
value0.270.2670.20.02740.3
Default prior: Uniform(min = 0.05, max = 1.5)
My take

Empirical lab observations of X. cheopis persistence on rat carcasses give 3–7 day infectious windows in temperate climates (so delta_R ~0.14–0.33) and as short as 1–2 days in hot tropical settings (delta_R 0.5–1.0). Didelot's 0.267 (3.7-day window) is a reasonable mid-temperate baseline. The upper bound of 1.5 in the lab prior is permissive — biologically I'd cap at 1.0 unless you have hot-summer carcass data. K&G's 0.0274 (36-day window) is suspect — that's longer than most fleas survive off-host; it likely absorbs other model deficiencies in their formulation.

Shared

Capacity & demography populations, births, deaths, infection durations

K_hhuman carrying capacity (population)count

Initial and equilibrium human population. The model uses this as the denominator for force-of-infection terms. Should match the historical population at outbreak onset.

scenariodefaultsdidelothistoricalkeeling-gilliganmodern-estimates
value—2.5e+05———
Default prior: Uniform(min = 500, max = 300000)
My take

There is no reason to fit this unless you genuinely don't know the population. The outbreaks dataset carries a population column for every outbreak (drawn from historical records / Krauer's curated dataset); the lab pins K_h from that by default. Fitting K_h trades off with beta_h and I_ini (the same death curve fits a range of (K, beta, seed) triples), so the posterior will be wider than it needs to be. Exception: medieval pre-census records where the historical population is genuinely uncertain — even then, prefer a tight informative prior over the default LogNormal.

Fixed
K_rrat carrying capacitycount

Initial and equilibrium rat population. Often taken as roughly equal to K_h (Didelot's convention: K_r = K_h = 250000 for Cairo). Unobservable directly — calibrated from human death dynamics, which is why pinning makes sense.

scenariodefaultsdidelothistoricalkeeling-gilliganmodern-estimates
value—2.5e+05———
Default prior: Uniform(min = 500, max = 300000)
My take

Pre-modern rat populations in urban settings were probably 1:1 with humans (rats follow humans for grain and waste). Modern cities have far lower ratios. Setting K_r = K_h is the safe convention for historical fits; setting it independently invites identifiability issues since K_r interacts with beta_r and rho in the rat force-of-infection. Only fit it if you have side information about rat densities (rare).

Fixed

Logistic per-day rat birth rate. Drives rat-population recovery during long outbreaks. Field-rat fecundity is high: 5–6 litters/year, 6–8 pups/litter, female maturity at 2 months. Per-day birth rate ~0.01–0.02 covers the realistic range.

Fixed
m_rplague resolution rate in ratsper day; 1/m_r = mean infectious duration

Rate at which infected rats resolve their infection (either recover or die). 1/m_r is the mean time from infection to outcome.

scenariodefaultsdidelothistoricalkeeling-gilliganmodern-estimates
value0.20.05560.040.054790.06
My take

Lab data on plague-infected Rattus norvegicus: 3–5 days from infection to death. So m_r should be 0.2–0.33. Most package scenarios use 0.04–0.06 (16–25-day window) — these are model-fit-derived values that absorb other dynamics. Didelot's m_r = m_h = 0.0556 is a simplifying choice the paper makes (assumes same plague timescale in both hosts) that isn't biologically defensible — rats die faster than untreated humans. For mechanistic interpretation, fix at 0.2 and let the model do the rest of the work.

Fixed
m_hplague resolution rate in humansper day; 1/m_h = mean infectious duration

Rate at which infected humans resolve to either recovery or death. 1/m_h is the mean disease duration.

scenariodefaultsdidelothistoricalkeeling-gilliganmodern-estimates
value0.080.05560.080.071230.15
My take

Clinical untreated bubonic plague: 10–18 days from onset to outcome (so m_h ~0.055–0.1). Didelot's 1/18 is at the slow end; historical = 0.08 (~12.5 days) is more typical. Pneumonic plague is much faster (2–3 days, m_h ~0.33–0.5) but the package doesn't separate routes — m_h is the bubonic default. Modern treatment shortens this dramatically, which is why modern-estimates uses 0.15.

Rarely fit
g_hhuman survival probabilityprobability ∈ [0, 1]

Probability that an infected human survives plague. 1 − g_h is the case fatality rate (CFR).

scenariodefaultsdidelothistoricalkeeling-gilliganmodern-estimates
value0.10.10.050.10.15
Default prior: Uniform(min = 0.05, max = 0.5)
My take

Untreated bubonic CFR is 50–90% (g_h 0.10–0.50). Pneumonic and septicemic are near-100% fatal (g_h ~0). Black-Death-era and Cairo records consistently show 85–95% CFR (historical g_h = 0.05, didelot g_h = 0.10). Modern treated cases survive 85%+ (modern g_h = 0.15). Weakly identified from death curves alone because beta_h, rho, and g_h all shift the death-count shape similarly — pin from clinical literature unless you have explicit case-vs-death data.

Reduces the birth rate of resistant rats vs susceptibles (iota = 0.75 ⇒ resistants reproduce 25% slower). Models the fitness cost of resistance.

Fixed

Initial conditions where the outbreak starts

I_iniinitial infected ratsabsolute count

Number of rats infected at t = 0. Absolute count, not a fraction (changed 2026-05 from S_ini fraction).

Default prior: Uniform(min = 10, max = 50000)
My take

Mathematically equivalent to changing time-of-introduction, so the posterior reflects "how many seeds + how long before records start." Historical context for picking the right scale: Eyam 1665 started from one ship-borne case (~1–10 rats); Cairo 1801 had ongoing endemic transmission (~3000–7000, matching Didelot's posterior fraction 0.005–0.07 of K_r); Black-Death cities arrived with grain-ship cargoes carrying many infected rats (103–104). The wide LogNormal(median 400) covers all of these with mass concentrated on the small-to-moderate seed counts that most outbreaks have. If you're winter-starting under strong seasonal suppression, you need huge seeds to ignite at all — the upper bound matters then.

R_iniinitial heritably-resistant ratsabsolute count

Number of rats with the heritable resistance trait at t = 0. Defaults to 0 — Didelot's all-susceptible start. Only meaningful when rat demography is active and can propagate the trait.

My take

Use only when deliberately exploring K&G-style trait propagation (Figure 5 replication). For real outbreak fits, leave at 0. There is no historical record of pre-existing rat resistance to seed a fit from.

Fixed
I_h_iniinitial infected humansabsolute count

Number of humans infected at t = 0. Useful when records start mid-outbreak with already-elevated death counts.

Default prior: Uniform(min = 0, max = 5000)
My take

Default 0 is correct when outbreak records start at first death (Eyam, Givry). Krauer's curated dataset includes mid-outbreak records (Cairo 1801 id 32, Cairo 1835) where deaths are already elevated on day 1 — fit it then. The Cairo 1835 records (Gaetani's military clinic) start with ~10–50 daily deaths, implying I_h_ini in the hundreds to low thousands. The Exponential(0.005) default puts mode at 0 (correct for records that start at outbreak ignition) while still putting enough tail mass on the hundreds-to-thousands range for mid-outbreak records.

Local
R_h_iniinitial recovered / immune humansabsolute count

Pre-existing immunity from prior plague exposure. Matters only for long outbreaks where herd immunity dynamics are visible in the death curve (>20% attack rate).

Default prior: Uniform(min = 0, max = 1000)
My take

Genuinely identifiable only when the outbreak reaches a large fraction of the population (20%+ attack rate) so the susceptible pool depletes visibly. Cairo at 13% attack rate — unidentifiable, pin at 0. Eyam at 25% — arguably identifiable since the village exhausted itself. For most Black-Death-era fits, residual immunity from prior waves is plausible but won't ever be teased out from death counts.

Observation model how counts come out of dynamics

kappaNegBinomial overdispersion (size)dimensionless, > 0

The "size" parameter of the NegBin observation likelihood. Large kappa → Poisson-like; small kappa → over-dispersed. Mean is set by p_obs · D_h + lambda_baseline · obs_period.

Default prior: Gamma(shape = 2, rate = 0.05)
My take

Historical mortality records are almost always over-dispersed (kappa < 10) because of clustered family deaths, scribal variability, and bursty reporting. Cleaner records (Cairo 1801 Desgenettes' military diary) tighten kappa toward 20+. Chronicle-based records (Florence 1400, medieval European cities) stay below 10. The Gamma(2, 0.05) prior puts mass on kappa ≈ 20–40 which is moderate overdispersion, and the data can pull it tighter or wider. If a fit returns kappa < 5, that's a record-quality warning — the diagnostic chip catches this.

Shared
p_obsobservation / ascertainment probabilityprobability ∈ [0, 1]

Fraction of true plague deaths that show up in the records. Models incomplete reporting and classification errors away from plague.

Default prior: Uniform(min = 0, max = 1)
My take

Didelot's 0.8 is a calibrated convention for clean military records and it's a reasonable default. Historical reality: Cairo 1801 (Desgenettes, professional army doctor) ≈ 0.8; Florence chronicle data ≈ 0.4–0.6 (mass graves, delayed reporting); Black-Death cities ≈ 0.3–0.6 (plague pits, scribal collapse). Partially confounded with lambda_baseline (both rescale the simulated series) — pin one, fit the other. Default convention: pin p_obs at 0.8, let lambda_baseline carry the misclassification signal.

Fixed
lambda_baselinebaseline non-plague deaths reported as plagueper day

Constant per-day count of non-plague deaths attributed to plague in the records. Captures (1) misclassification — other diseases attributed to plague during scares — and (2) ambient background mortality lumped into outbreak counts.

Default prior: Exponential(rate = 0.1)
My take

Empirically diagnostic of record quality. Cairo 1801 (Desgenettes' careful military records) posterior ≈ 1; Cairo 1835 (Gaetani, broader civilian clinical dataset) posterior ≈ 20. Florence 1400 chronicle data should run higher still. For a fresh outbreak without prior pilots: Exponential(0.1) (mean 10) is conservative — concentrated near zero but with mass out to tens. If the posterior pins against the prior's mode (near 0), your records are clean; if it pushes into the tens, your records are murky.

Local
obs_periodobservation aggregation windowdays; integer

How many days of model output get summed between data points. Most outbreaks have daily records (obs_period = 1); London 1563 has weekly Bills of Mortality (obs_period = 7). The model accumulates D_h over this window before resetting.

My take

Lives in the outbreaks dataset metadata. Pin from the outbreak; the lab errors if you try to mix cadences in one fit. Don't be tempted to coarsen daily data to weekly to mix with London 1563 — you'd lose information without gaining anything.

Fixed
seasonalper-day delta_R multiplier vectorlength-T_max numeric

Per-day multiplier on delta_R. Encodes seasonal modulation of carcass decay (and hence carcass-mediated transmission). Defaults to rep(1, T_max) (no seasonality). Length must match the number of simulation days.

My take

Not inferred — it's input data. Build from climate records via the with_briere_seasonal() packer wrapper (temperature → Brière function → multiplier), or from a hand-built annual sine via with_alpha_seasonal(). Seasonal forcing's strength can be inferred via a fitted alpha reparameterization (see the Barcelona mechanistic vignette). The lab v1 doesn't expose alpha/Brière; seasonal stays at 1.

Fixed

What varies where cohort · outbreak · within-outbreak structure

Universally fixed (across all scenarios and outbreaks)

  • iota = 0.75 — resistance fecundity cost. Documented as “always fixed, never inferred.”
  • tau = 1 — discretization timestep. Match data cadence via obs_period, not tau.

Fixed per scenario, shared across an outbreak cohort

Biological parameters that don’t vary between Eyam-1665 and Cairo-1801 because they’re properties of plague itself: m_r, m_h, g_r, rho, p, r_r, r_h, d_r, d_h. They do vary across scenarios (Black Death era vs modern) but not within a scenario.

Fit at the cohort level, shared across outbreaks

  • beta_r — rat-side transmission biology.
  • rho — flea redistribution.
  • kappa — overdispersion, when sources are similar quality.
  • delta_R — baseline carcass decay (unmodulated case).

Fit per outbreak

  • beta_h — varies city-to-city (housing, sanitation).
  • I_ini — outbreak ignition seed.
  • lambda_baseline — record quality, varies by scribe / era.
  • I_h_ini — if records start mid-outbreak.

Pinned per outbreak from metadata (not fit)

  • K_h — known historical population.
  • K_r — defaults to K_h.
  • obs_period — record cadence (daily / weekly).
  • seasonal — when constructed from climate data.

Within-outbreak variation

The only parameter that varies within a single outbreak is seasonal — a length-T vector of per-day multipliers on delta_R. Everything else is constant over the outbreak’s duration. Time-varying transmission rates are not currently supported without extending the model.

Scenario philosophy when to pick which

defaults

Didelot/K&G blend. Sensible biological values without commitment to a specific outbreak. Good for experimentation; not calibrated to any one dataset.

My take

Use for sandbox exploration. Don’t fit real outbreak data starting from this — pick the era-appropriate scenario.

didelot

Cairo 1801 posterior means from Didelot et al. 2017. Includes K_h = K_r = 250000 and p_obs = 0.8 baked in. beta_I = 0.0215 non-zero (pneumonic component). Rat demography off (r_r = d_r = 0) for short timescale.

My take

The right starting point for any short urban outbreak (~150 days). The fixed K and p_obs are the calibrations the paper depends on; if you use a different outbreak with this scenario, update K from the outbreak metadata. Don’t fit Eyam from here — rat demography matters there.

historical

Black-Death-era (~1347–1700). Higher d_h (life expectancy ~30 years), lower g_h (5% survival), higher r_r, lower delta_R (cooler climates).

My take

The right starting point for medieval / early modern European fits (Givry 1348, Florence 1400, Eyam 1665). The 95% CFR reflects the overwhelming clinical evidence from that era. Watch out for cold-season starts — if seasonality matters, add the seasonal vector or use the Brière wrapper from packer_helpers.R.

keeling-gilligan

Keeling & Gilligan 2000 spatial model adapted to the carcass formulation via the flea quasi-equilibrium. Tiny beta_r (0.0863) and high rho (10) because their carrying capacity is per-block.

My take

This is NOT an outbreak-fitting scenario. It exists to replicate K&G figures and to drive the cellular-automaton tools (R/ca.R). The parameter values don’t translate to population-level historical fits — don’t use them for that.

modern-estimates

Contemporary post-antibiotic-era plague. Shorter durations (m_h = 0.15, 6.7-day disease), higher g_h = 0.15 (15% untreated CFR survival).

My take

Use for Madagascar 2017, US Southwest sporadic cases, etc. Modern surveillance gives much better p_obs — consider pinning it at 0.95 rather than 0.8 if you’re fitting modern data.
